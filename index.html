üìç Objectives:

The objectives involve mastering frog dissection, understanding comparative anatomy, and applying knowledge to broader biological concepts. Students aim to excel in amphibian anatomy, appreciate organ system diversity, and connect theory with practice.

Understanding Comparative Anatomy

Students should be able to compare the organ systems of frogs to those of other representative animals, recognizing both general and unique characteristics. This involves describing how the frog's organ systems contribute to its adaptation to its environment and identifying similarities and differences with organ systems in other animals. This objective encourages students to appreciate the diversity of anatomical structures across species.

Proficient Frog Dissection Skills

By the end of the dissection unit, students should demonstrate proficient skills in frog dissection, including the accurate identification and manipulation of key anatomical structures such as the digestive system, respiratory system, circulatory system, and reproductive organs. Students should be able to articulate the purpose and function of each organ system in the frog, highlighting the similarities and differences with human physiology.

Applying Knowledge to Biological Concepts

Apply the knowledge gained from frog dissection to broader biological concepts. Students should be able to explain the physiological processes involved in digestion, respiration, circulation, and reproduction, drawing connections between the frog's anatomy and its overall biology. This objective aims to deepen the understanding of biological principles through hands-on experience with frog dissection and its application to broader biological contexts.

    Step 1

    Put on safety goggles, gloves, and a laboratory gown.

  2. 02.

    Step 2

    Place a frog on a dissection tray. To determine the frog‚Äôs sex, look at the hand digits or fingers on its forelegs. A male frog usually has thick pads on its ‚Äúthumbs,‚Äù which is one external difference between the sexes, as shown in Figure 1 below. Male frogs are also usually smaller than female frogs. Observe several frogs to see the difference between males and females.

  3. 03.

    Step 3

    Using Figure 2, locate and identify the external features of the head. Find the mouth, external nares, tympani, eyes, and nictating membranes.

  4. 04.

    Step 4

    Turn the frog on its back and pin down the legs. Cut the hinges of the mount and open it wide. Using Figure 3, locate and identify the structures inside the mouth. Use a probe to help find each part: the vomerine teeth, the maxillary teeth, the internal nares, the tongue, the openings to the Eustachian tubes, the esophagus, the pharynx, and the slit-like glottis.

  5. 05.

    Step 5

    Look for the opening to the frog‚Äôs cloaca, located between the hind legs. Use forceps to lift the skin and use scissors to cut along the center of the body from the cloaca to the lip. Turn back the skin, cut toward the side at each leg, and pin the skin flat. Figure 4 shows how to make these cuts.

  6. 06.

    Step 6

    Lift and cut through the muscles and breast bone to open up the body cavity. If your frog is a female, the abdominal cavity may be filled with dark-colored eggs. If so, remove the eggs on one side so you can see the organs underlying them.

  7. 07.

    Step 7

    Figure 5, locate and identify the organs of the digestive system: esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, gallbladder, liver, spleen, pancreas, and cloaca.

  8. 08.

    Step 8

    Figure 6, identify the parts of the circulatory and respiratory systems that are in the chest cavity. Find the left atrium, right atrium, and ventricle of the heart. Find an artery attached to the heart and another artery near the backbone. Find a vein near one of the shoulders. Find the two lungs.

  9. 09.

    Step 9

    Use a probe and scissors to lift and remove the intestines and liver. Use the diagram on the next page to identify the parts of the urinary and reproductive systems. Remove the peritoneal membrane, which is connective tissue that lies on top of the red kidneys. Observe the yellow fat bodies that are attached to the kidneys. Find the ureters, urinary bladder, testes and sperm ducts of male frogs, and the ovaries, oviducts, and uteri of female frogs.

  10. 10.

    Step 10

    Dispose of your materials according to the directions from your teacher.

  11. 11.

    Step 11

    Clean up your work area and wash your hands before leaving the lab.
